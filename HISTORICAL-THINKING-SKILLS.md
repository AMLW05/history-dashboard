# Historical Thinking Skills Progression
## FTCC History Program

**Purpose**: Layer historical thinking skills progressively across courses to develop advanced historical analysis capabilities

---

## Program Philosophy

**Think Like a Historian** - Students learn historical thinking by DOING historical analysis throughout:
- Analyzing primary and secondary sources
- Making evidence-based arguments
- Contextualizing events across time and space
- Evaluating multiple perspectives
- Building from foundational to advanced skills

---

## Skills Ladder

### HIS-131: American History I (FOUNDATIONAL)
**Skills Focus**: Source identification, basic chronology, descriptive analysis

**Skills Elements**:
- ✅ **Source Analysis**: Distinguish primary from secondary sources
- ✅ **Chronological Reasoning**: Identify cause-effect relationships, sequence events
- ✅ **Contextualization**: Place events in their historical time and place
- ✅ **Comparison**: Compare different regions/groups/time periods
- ✅ **Argumentation**: Construct basic thesis statements with evidence
- ✅ **Data Literacy**: Read and interpret basic historical data

**Skill Development by Module**:
- **Modules 1-2**: Identify sources, describe context
- **Modules 3-4**: Analyze causes and effects
- **Modules 5-6**: Compare perspectives, evaluate evidence
- **Modules 7-8**: Construct arguments, synthesize information

**Assessments**:
- LA1: Key terms (identification)
- LA2: Multiple choice (application)
- LA3: Timeline/comparison (analysis)
- Discussions: Basic argument with evidence
- Portfolio: Progressive synthesis

---

### HIS-132: American History II (INTERMEDIATE)
**Skills Focus**: Evaluation, synthesis, data literacy, contemporary connections

**Skills Elements**:
- ✅ **Source Analysis**: Evaluate credibility, bias, and perspective
- ✅ **Chronological Reasoning**: Analyze continuity and change over time
- ✅ **Contextualization**: Connect historical context to present issues
- ✅ **Comparison**: Make comparative judgments across eras
- ✅ **Argumentation**: Develop complex thesis with counterarguments
- ✅ **Data Literacy**: Interpret quantitative data, create data-driven arguments

**Skill Development by Module**:
- **Modules 1-2**: Evaluate multiple sources, assess reliability
- **Modules 3-4**: Analyze historical data, make data-driven claims
- **Modules 5-6**: Synthesize across themes and time periods
- **Modules 7-8**: Make historical connections to contemporary issues

**Featured Activities**:
- **Module 3 Data Analysis**: Progressive Era reform statistics
- **Module 6 Data Analysis**: Civil Rights Movement impact metrics
- **Portfolio Project**: Synthesis of historical understanding across 8 modules

**Assessments**:
- Enhanced discussions with historiographical perspectives
- Data analysis activities with interpretation
- Portfolio with synthesis and contemporary reflection

## Transferable Skills Development

Students develop these professional skills through historical thinking:

**Critical Thinking**:
- Evaluate sources for reliability and bias
- Identify logical fallacies
- Make evidence-based judgments

**Information Literacy**:
- Research effectively
- Assess credibility of sources
- Cite appropriately

**Communication**:
- Construct clear arguments
- Support claims with evidence
- Write for different audiences

**Civic Engagement**:
- Understand contemporary issues through historical lens
- Engage with diverse perspectives
- Make informed decisions as citizens

**Data Analysis**:
- Interpret quantitative information
- Identify patterns and trends
- Create data-driven arguments

## Implementation Notes

**Quality Matters Alignment**:
- Clear learning objectives for each skill level
- Aligned assessments measuring skill development
- Regular feedback on skill progression
- Rubrics targeting specific skills

**Scaffolding Strategy**:
- Skills introduced with modeling and examples
- Practice activities with immediate feedback
- Increasing complexity across modules
- Synthesis opportunities in capstone assessments

**Assessment Design**:
- Formative: Learning activities with auto-grading
- Summative: Discussions, portfolio pieces, exams
- Authentic: Real historical analysis tasks
- Progressive: Building complexity throughout semester
