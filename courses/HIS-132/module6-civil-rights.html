Question 1

2.5 points
Question Text
According to the voter registration data, how did Black voter registration in the South change after the Voting Rights Act of 1965?
Answer Options (Enter all 4 in Canvas)
A It increased dramatically from 29% (1960) to 62% (1968), demonstrating the law's effectiveness CORRECT
B It declined because the law was not enforced
C It remained unchanged at 29%
D It decreased to 3% as in 1940
Feedback (Enter in Canvas feedback fields)
Correct Answer Feedback
Correct! The Voting Rights Act's federal oversight and ban on literacy tests produced immediate, measurable results - registration more than doubled in just 3 years (29% to 62%).
Incorrect Answer Feedback
Compare voter registration rates before (1960: 29%) and after (1968: 62%) the Voting Rights Act of 1965. What direction did the numbers move?

Question 3
Canvas Type: Multiple Answer
2.5 points
Question Text
The school desegregation data shows only 2% of Black students attended integrated schools by 1964 - TEN years after Brown v. Board. Select ALL factors that explain this slow progress. (Select all that apply)
Answer Options (Mark multiple correct in Canvas)
A Southern states engaged in "massive resistance" to court orders CORRECT
B The federal government initially lacked enforcement mechanisms CORRECT
C White flight and neighborhood segregation limited integration CORRECT
D The Supreme Court immediately enforced desegregation nationwide
Feedback
Correct Answer Feedback
Correct! Brown v. Board declared segregation unconstitutional but provided no enforcement timeline. Southern resistance (closing schools, "freedom of choice" plans) combined with federal reluctance to intervene slowed progress dramatically.
Incorrect Answer Feedback
The Supreme Court's Brown II decision (1955) called for desegregation "with all deliberate speed" but provided no specific timeline or enforcement mechanism.

Answer Options (Mark multiple correct in Canvas)
A It banned literacy tests that had been used to prevent Black registration CORRECT
B It allowed federal oversight in states with histories of discrimination CORRECT
C Registration numbers show immediate, dramatic improvement after passage CORRECT

Feedback
Correct Answer Feedback
Correct! The Voting Rights Act's effectiveness came from its specific provisions: banning discriminatory tests, requiring federal preclearance for voting law changes, and sending federal registrars to enforce compliance.
