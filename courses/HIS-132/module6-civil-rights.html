Instructions: Analyze Civil Rights movement data including voter registration, desegregation timeline, legislative victories, and protest participation.

Black Voter Registration in the South

Registration rising from 3% (1940) to 29% (1960) to 62% (1968) after Voting Rights Act

School Desegregation Progress

Only 2% of Black students in integrated schools by 1964, rising to 32% by 1968

Major Civil Rights Legislation

Civil Rights Act of 1964

Banned discrimination in employment and public accommodations

Voting Rights Act of 1965

Protected voting rights; banned literacy tests

Fair Housing Act of 1968

Banned housing discrimination

Analysis Questions

Q1: How did Black voter registration in the South change after the Voting Rights Act of 1965?
Answer: Registration jumped from 29% (1960) to 62% (1968), demonstrating the law's effectiveness in protecting voting rights
Q2: What does school desegregation data reveal about Southern resistance to Brown v. Board?
Answer: Only 2% of Black students attended integrated schools by 1964 (10 years after Brown), showing massive resistance to court orders
Q3: Which Civil Rights law had the most immediate measurable impact on voting?
Answer: Voting Rights Act of 1965, which banned literacy tests and led to dramatic increase in Black voter registration
Q4: What pattern do you see in protest participation over time?
Answer: Participation grew massively from local (Montgomery) to national (March on Washington with 250,000), showing movement's expanding reach
Q5: Why might school desegregation have occurred more slowly than voter registration?
Answer: School integration required overcoming local resistance, white flight, and lack of federal enforcement, while Voting Rights Act had federal oversight
Q6: What do the three major civil rights laws (1964, 1965, 1968) collectively address?
Answer: They comprehensively banned discrimination in public accommodations, voting, employment, and housing
Q7: How do protest numbers demonstrate the movement's power?
Answer: Massive participation (250,000 at March on Washington) created political pressure forcing federal action on civil rights
Q8: What does the 10-year gap between Brown v. Board (1954) and Civil Rights Act (1964) suggest?
Answer: Legal victories alone were insufficient; grassroots protests and direct action were necessary to force federal legislation
Q9: Which data source best demonstrates the effectiveness of federal enforcement?
Answer: Voter registration data after 1965, showing that federal protection under Voting Rights Act produced immediate, measurable results
Q10: How do these data help historians understand the Civil Rights Movement's success?
Answer: They reveal that legal changes required grassroots mobilization, federal enforcement, and persistence over decades to achieve measurable impact
