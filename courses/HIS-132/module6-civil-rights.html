Quiz Questions (10 Questions, 25 Points Total)

Q1 Multiple Choice (2.5 pts)
According to the voter registration data, how did Black voter registration in the South change after the Voting Rights Act of 1965?
A It increased dramatically from 29% (1960) to 62% (1968), demonstrating the law's effectiveness
B It declined because the law was not enforced
C It remained unchanged at 29%
D It decreased to 3% as in 1940
The Voting Rights Act's federal oversight and ban on literacy tests produced immediate, measurable results - registration more than doubled in just 3 years.
Q2 Numeric (2.5 pts)
Based on the voter registration data, calculate the percentage point increase in Black voter registration from 1940 to 1968.
Answer: 59 percentage points (62% - 3% = 59)
Most of this gain came after the Voting Rights Act (1965).
Q3 Multiple Answer (2.5 pts)
The school desegregation data shows only 2% of Black students attended integrated schools by 1964 - TEN years after Brown v. Board. Select ALL factors that explain this slow progress. (Select all that apply)
A Southern states engaged in "massive resistance" to court orders
B The federal government initially lacked enforcement mechanisms
C White flight and neighborhood segregation limited integration
D The Supreme Court immediately enforced desegregation nationwide
Brown v. Board declared segregation unconstitutional but provided no enforcement timeline. Southern resistance combined with federal reluctance to intervene slowed progress dramatically.
Q4 Ordering (2.5 pts)
Place the following Civil Rights legislation in chronological order (earliest to latest).
  1. Civil Rights Act - banned discrimination in employment and public accommodations (1964)
  2. Voting Rights Act - protected voting rights and banned literacy tests (1965)
  3. Fair Housing Act - banned housing discrimination (1968)
The three major Civil Rights laws were passed in quick succession: 1964, 1965, 1968. Together they addressed the major areas of legal discrimination.
Q5 Categorization (2.5 pts)
Compare the voter registration and school desegregation data. Categorize each statement about the difference in progress rates.
Why Voter Registration Increased Faster
Voting Rights Act included federal oversight and enforcement provisions
Federal registrars could be sent to counties with low Black registration
Clear metric (registration percentage) made progress measurable and enforceable
Why School Desegregation Was Slower
Integration required changing local school systems and overcoming white flight
Residential segregation meant neighborhood schools remained segregated
Local control of education allowed continued resistance to integration
Q6 Matching (2.5 pts)
Match each Civil Rights law with the specific barrier to equality it addressed.
Civil Rights Act of 1964Segregated lunch counters, hotels, and employment discrimination
Voting Rights Act of 1965Literacy tests, poll taxes, and registration barriers
Fair Housing Act of 1968Discriminatory practices by realtors, banks, and landlords
Brown v. Board of Education (1954)Legally mandated separate schools for Black and white children
Each law targeted specific mechanisms of discrimination. Together with Brown, they dismantled legal segregation.
Q7 Multiple Choice (2.5 pts)
The protest participation data shows growth from 50,000 (Montgomery, local) to 250,000 (March on Washington, national). What does this pattern reveal about the Civil Rights Movement's development?
A The movement expanded from local protests to massive national demonstrations, creating political pressure for federal action
B Protest participation declined over time as people lost interest
C All protests remained small and local throughout the period
D The federal government organized and funded all protests
The movement's growth from local to national demonstrated its expanding reach and created irresistible pressure for federal legislation.
Q8 Multiple Answer (2.5 pts)
Based on ALL the data, select ALL conclusions about what made the Voting Rights Act of 1965 particularly effective. (Select all that apply)
A It banned literacy tests that had been used to prevent Black registration
B It allowed federal oversight in states with histories of discrimination
C Registration numbers show immediate, dramatic improvement after passage
D It relied entirely on Southern states to enforce their own compliance
The Act's success came from its specific provisions: banning discriminatory tests, requiring federal preclearance, and sending federal registrars to enforce compliance.
Q9 Categorization (2.5 pts)
The data reveals a 10-year gap between Brown v. Board (1954) and the Civil Rights Act (1964). Categorize each factor.
Why Legal Victories Alone Were Insufficient
Courts could declare laws unconstitutional but couldn't force compliance
Southern states found ways to resist or circumvent court orders
Federal government was reluctant to intervene without public pressure
Why Grassroots Protests Were Necessary
Mass demonstrations created political pressure and media attention
Direct action (sit-ins, marches) exposed injustice to national audience
Protests in Birmingham (1963) shocked nation and spurred legislation
Q10 Multiple Choice (2.5 pts)
Analyzing all the data together, which statement BEST describes what the evidence reveals about Civil Rights Movement success?
A Success required the combination of legal challenges, grassroots protests, and federal legislation with enforcement mechanisms
B A single Supreme Court decision immediately ended all discrimination
C Progress occurred automatically without organized resistance
D The data shows no measurable progress was made during this period
The data reveals that multiple strategies working together produced change: Brown provided legal foundation, protests created pressure, legislation provided tools, and enforcement made laws effective.
