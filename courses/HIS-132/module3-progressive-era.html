Strikes increasing from 1,900 (1890) to 4,450 (1904), demonstrating growing labor militancy

Analysis Questions

Q1: Based on immigration data, what year saw peak immigration before WWI?
Answer: 1907, with 1,285,000 immigrants
Q2: What pattern do you notice in the states that granted women's suffrage BEFORE the 19th Amendment?
Answer: Western states granted suffrage first (Wyoming 1890, Colorado 1893, etc.), while Southern and Eastern states resisted until federal amendment
Q3: How did the number of labor strikes change from 1890 to 1904?
Answer: Strikes more than doubled from 1,900 to 4,450, showing increased worker activism
Q4: Why might immigration have declined sharply after 1907?
Answer: Economic recession (Panic of 1907), growing restrictions on immigration, and outbreak of WWI in Europe (1914)
Q5: What does the child labor data reveal about the success of Progressive reforms by 1900?
Answer: Despite reform efforts, 1.75 million children still worked, showing limited success of child labor laws by 1900
Q6: Which region was MOST resistant to women's suffrage before 1920?
Answer: The South, where most states did not grant suffrage until forced by the 19th Amendment
Q7: What relationship do you see between immigration and labor strikes?
Answer: Both increased during same period; immigrant workers often participated in strikes despite facing discrimination
Q8: Why might Western states have been more willing to grant women's suffrage?
Answer: Frontier societies had fewer traditional gender restrictions; suffrage seen as way to attract settlers and promote 'civilization'
Q9: What does rising labor militancy suggest about worker satisfaction during industrialization?
Answer: Workers were increasingly dissatisfied with wages, conditions, and treatment, leading to more organized resistance
Q10: How do these data sources help historians understand the Progressive Era?
Answer: They reveal patterns of social change, resistance to reform, regional differences, and gap between reformers' goals and actual outcomes
